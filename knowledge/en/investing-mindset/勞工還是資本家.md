> **In 30 seconds**: James compresses Chapter 1 of *The Ten-Billion-Dollar Investment Lecture* into one question — **are you a laborer or a capitalist?** Traditional schooling spends 20+ years training you into a competent laborer, but never tells you that being rich is a birthright. The first step out of the rat race isn't a technique — it's **awareness**: your salary buys you today; investing buys your financial freedom in 30 years.[^1]

## Tenant farmer and landlord: the metaphor that runs through the course

The tenant farmer wakes at 3–4am, bends over the paddy until dark, and teaches his child "come learn to farm with me." The landlord wakes naturally, collects rent, serves society, and tells his child "study whatever you like." What separates them isn't a few generations — it's **an entire class**.[^1]

- **How a tenant becomes a landlord**: save part of the rice harvested, accumulate, buy land, hire others to farm it.
- **How a laborer becomes a capitalist**: save part of the salary into [[指數基金|index funds]], accumulate until the **compound income from assets exceeds your labor income** — that is financial freedom.

James uses himself and Buffett as proof: his lifetime salary is a rounding error next to his investment gains; Buffett's pre-50 net worth is just 1% of today's — 99% was earned by capital after 50.[^1]

## "Being rich is a birthright" — a forgotten notification

Why does the laborer's fate persist? Because of one missing act — **being told**. Just as women and Black Americans were once conditioned to believe they had no right to vote, until someone awakened that awareness, the logic of wealth is identical: no one ever told you "being rich is a birthright" — only "study hard, work hard." Capitalism already designed a fair, open road to wealth, but the entire education system exists to train competent laborers. **A capitalist doesn't need to be trained — only to be told.**[^1]

## The rat race and two traps

A laborer's life is a loop: **earn → pay tax → repay debt → spend → earn again**. James names the two traps young people most easily fall into:[^1]

- **The "sacred mountain" tech job**: praised as enviable, but often feels like a labor camp from the inside — body, family, and spirit crushed under the mountain.
- **Buying a house**: a house is a high-risk asset. In layoff waves, owing a large mortgage every month with no income is "mortgage backlash." His words: **"Better to raise a child than to raise a house."** Once you're rich enough that buying a home is like buying slippers, you can rent or buy any mansion — with the bank's money.

The first step out of the rat race is **never a technique — it's awareness**: seeing that the whole script (study, good job, buy house and car, die a few years after retiring) was *designed*, not natural law.

## Material energy vs. spiritual energy

"Simple living" isn't about saving money — it's about **thinking before you spend**: ask "will I still use this in six months?" All material consumption eventually depreciates into junk; all spiritual consumption (a good meal, a trip, time with family) almost never disappears. Past a wealth threshold, deliberately redirect spending from the material to the spiritual. This is why lottery winners often end up poor — their spiritual energy never caught up with sudden material energy. Where does spiritual energy come from? **Reading a lot, staying calm, and not being jerked around by the market.**[^1]

> 💡 **"You in heaven, the debt at the bank."** The ideal is to live mostly on *borrowed* money (credit, [[質押借款|pledged loans]]), maximizing the conversion of assets into spiritual energy — rather than hoarding hundreds of millions in cash you never actually spend.

## Unemployment isn't an accident — it's capitalism's design; and when the AI tsunami hits, board the ark

00555 states the "the rat race is designed" point even more bluntly:[^2]

- **Unemployment is a "necessary condition" of capitalism, not a malfunction**. "The capitalist system was designed by capitalists; **unemployment is a necessary condition of capitalism** — because it manufactures urgency in workers, so they toil obediently." In other words, keeping some people perpetually at risk of unemployment — and everyone else afraid not to comply — is precisely the fuel that runs the system (echoing the "rat race" above and [[別為錢賣命]]).
- **The AI tsunami leaves one way out**: "Before the AI tsunami arrives, **only by boarding the 'capitalist' ark can you ride out the flood**." As companies and jobs are progressively replaced by AI (see [[從擇時操作到打死不賣|the AI-layoff observations in 00558/00562]]), you want to be a **shareholder owning these AI companies**, not a worker being made redundant. Humans, meanwhile, distinguish themselves from AI through **thinking, the uniquely human soul, and craftsman's spirit**.
- **Geopolitical footnote**: James argues that Chinese-heritage societies (China, Taiwan, Korea), thanks to the traditional culture of "diligence and saving," are among the few Global-South economies with a chance to escape "enslavement"; Taiwan, with tiny land and population, escaped via its semiconductor leverage (though he calls it "only half-freed," and TSMC's success "an accident, absolutely, from America's point of view"). This strand is geopolitical, bearing on [[美元霸權與石油美元|who gets to be capitalism's master]]; noted only in brief here.

### Member stories: from worker to "the helmsman of my own life"

Two letters in 00555 are the best living textbook for "worker → capitalist":[^2]

- **A 54-year-old who rode through the 2022 bear market**: during the pandemic he held a pile of individual stocks, dropped 30–40%, and "often woke in the morning gripped by inexplicable terror" before realizing he had to change. He met CLEC in late 2021, fully adopted the allocation, rode through the 2022 US bear market, and **saw his assets go from "negative" to doubling within three and a half years**; now 54, he's begun planning retirement. James replied: "You're a living textbook — from zero to ready-to-retire; I hope it can be replicated for every member — escape the rat race, reclaim your freedom and your soul. **Here, where the heart is at peace, is home.**"
- **A member who crossed the "coast point" (@EiffelBurkins)**: after nearly three years of "mindlessly buying the Nasdaq ETF and never selling," his passive income soon overtook his salary; one day he realized his **assets had crossed his planned "coast point"** — **no more contributions needed; compounding alone will glide him to the finish**. He describes going "from a worker with no financial security to the helmsman of my own life; others can no longer control me." He wants no luxury cars or mansions, but **wealth is the freedom to choose** — for generations. His closing line could serve as this page's epigraph: **"Wealth brings not outward show, but silent confidence. Money talks, but wealth keeps silent."** (On the power of knowing who you are, see [[身份認同與長期投資]].)
