> **In 30 seconds**: James says "[[勞工還是資本家|spiritual energy]] comes from reading a lot." "No patience, can't understand it, no time" are all excuses — a foundation of learning to invest is absorbing correct knowledge so you can judge whether financial news and pundits are actually true. **If you don't get it the first time, read it repeatedly; the more you read, the more it clicks.**[^1]

## Six areas to cover

1. Market history 2. Global finance 3. Central-bank monetary policy 4. Market greed — bubbles 5. Market fear — bottoms 6. Investing psychology.[^1]

## Getting started (easy → advanced)

- ***A Random Walk Down Wall Street*** (Burton Malkiel) + *The Ten Golden Rules*
- ***The Four Pillars of Investing*** (William Bernstein)
- ***Stocks for the Long Run*** (Jeremy Siegel)
- ***Enough*** (John Bogle)
- ***The Investment Answer***
- ***Winning the Loser's Game*** (Charles Ellis)
- **The Index Revolution** (the Buffett-endorsed index approach)
- Peter Lynch: ***One Up on Wall Street*** & ***Beating the Street***

## Investing operations

- ***The Most Important Thing*** (Howard Marks)
- ***The Only Three Questions That Still Count*** (Ken Fisher)
- ***Debunkery*** (Ken Fisher)
- ***Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques*** (Steve Nison)

## Philosophy & macro trends

- ***21st Century Monetary Policy*** (Ben Bernanke)
- ***The Age of Turbulence*** & ***The Map and the Territory*** (Alan Greenspan)
- ***Business Cycles: History, Theory and Investment Reality***
- ***Mastering the Market Cycle*** (Howard Marks)

> The list centers on index-based, long-term, passive investing. Technical-analysis titles (e.g. candlesticks) are included so you can "understand what others are saying," not as a CLEC recommendation to trade — the core is still [[為什麼是納斯達克100|buy the Nasdaq and never sell]].
