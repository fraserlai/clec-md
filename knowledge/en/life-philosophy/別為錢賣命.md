> **In 30 seconds**: At **15×** annual expenses you should start asking "can I retire?"; at **25×** you can retire and do something more meaningful (be with family). If your assets are already past **50×** and you're still working for money, James is blunt: that's "**trading your life for garbage**" — the surplus you can't spend, and it just becomes inheritance. The core logic: outdoing Buffett is about **living long enough**, not earning fast enough — and the most effective way to extend your life is to stop working for money.[^1]

## How many times over is enough to stop?

CLEC's retirement thresholds are a staircase (exact multiples and local conversions in [[退休需要幾倍年開銷|the retirement multiple]] and [[退休需要多少錢|how much you need]]):[^1]

- **15× (minimum)**: you meet the minimum retirement condition — your mind should **start asking "should I retire?"**
- **25×**: you can retire and spend your life on something more meaningful — being with family. Assets keep growing; in another 5–10 years it often doubles to 50×.
- **Past 50× and still working for money**: James puts it bluntly — "you're literally trading your life for garbage, because however much more money you make, that inheritance is garbage to you." Many people **already have enough and could retire, yet keep dragging an exhausted body to work** — not worth it.[^1]

## Why "live ten more years" beats "work ten more years"

James breaks wealth into two factors: **start investing very early, or live long enough** — "the most important thing in investing is staying in the market **long enough**, not **fast enough**." "Work hard" is never on the list.[^2]

- You can't go back to investing at age 7 (that time is gone), so "live longer" is the only lever left. **The wealth from living 10 more years beats working 10 more years.**
- And the most effective way to extend your life is to **stop working for money** — take your body and time back.
- Better "live to 100 at 8%/yr" than "only 10 years left at 20%/yr." Crashing from driving too fast is worse than staying in steadily.[^2]

## Life > money

- **Don't trade a healthy body for money**, and **don't trade the money you need for money you don't need** (over-reaching for money you can't even spend, while risking the principal you actually need).[^3]
- "If you lose your life over working a few more months, is it worth it?" James's reminder: **go get a health check** (low-dose CT, gastroscopy, colonoscopy, the cancer screens) — living long matters more than working more. By the time cancer is found, it's too late to learn this.[^1]
- A Tibetan monk spends a week building a mandala from fine sand, then sweeps it away with a broom and pours it into the Hudson River: **all glory returns to dust**. Money, in the end, is all inheritance, all "emptiness"; let go of what should be let go.[^2]

## Leave the rat race early, don't just run faster

CLEC's mindset is **how to leave the worker's rat race early**, not to keep burrowing deeper and running faster thinking speed makes more money — "money doesn't come from a worker's effort" (see [[勞工還是資本家|Worker or capitalist]]).[^1]

James also names a kind of self-enslavement — "**a worker with a capitalist's head**" — echoing a boss who says "if workers dislike the pay, go buy stock yourselves." His retort: then why don't *you* (the capitalist) just buy stock and skip your own salary and bonus? **Don't let yourself be enslaved.**[^1]

## Work can be an option, not a sentence

Retiring doesn't mean doing nothing — it means **work becomes optional**: do it if you want to, find it interesting, or can help people; not for money.[^3]

- James says if he opened a café, the point would be **joy** — he'd be happy losing $100k a month; **if you're opening a shop to make money, don't bother.** (He also consistently advises "don't start a business" — too draining, and if it fails you're wiped out.)[^1]
- "There's always someone who likes to work — just make sure it isn't you" — no need to worry "if everyone retires, how does capitalism run?"[^1]
- 00570's member SF (42, a shift-rotation technician) originally just wanted a non-shift job; he ran the numbers and found **the difference between working on and retiring was small**, so he retired outright — exactly "once you hit the threshold, stop selling your life for money" (see [[該不該辭職或提早退休|Should I quit or retire early]]).[^3]
