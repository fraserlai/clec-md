> **In 30 seconds**: Two steps. **First the math — "can I afford it"**: how many times your annual expenses you have, and whether cash flow holds ([[退休需要幾倍年開銷|the retirement multiple]], [[十五年現金流|15 years of cash flow]]). **Then the values — "is it worth it"**: once you hit 15–25×, the marginal point of working for money is small — stop trading your life for garbage ([[別為錢賣命|don't sell your life for money]]). Before switching to an easier job, run the numbers — often "keep working" and "retire" differ little; **full-time investing is fine but don't make it a new rat race**; if the workplace is toxic, leave.[^1]

## Situational variants (how people actually ask it)

- "Should I switch to a non-shift / less exhausting job, or just retire?"
- "Should I quit to invest full-time / live off investing?"
- "I'm already financially free — should I keep working?"
- "I've run the numbers and I seem to be past the threshold, but I just don't dare retire — what do I do?"
- "My company has a bad boss and toxic labor relations — should I leave?"

## Two questions: first "can I afford it," then "is it worth it"

- **Can I afford it**: a pure financial question — see [[退休需要幾倍年開銷|the retirement multiple]] (15× is the threshold; the multiple scales with your high-dividend yield) and [[十五年現金流|15 years of cash flow]] (workers keep an unemployed-to-reemployed emergency fund; retirees need 15 years of cash flow).
- **Is it worth it**: a values question — see [[別為錢賣命|don't sell your life for money]]. James's stance is clear: past the threshold, dragging "an exhausted body" to work for money isn't worth it, because the surplus just becomes inheritance — and **living 10 more years beats working 10 more years**.[^2]

## Want an easier job? Run the difference first

00570's member **SF (42, a traditional three-shift-rotation technician)** originally just wanted to switch to a non-shift job. He ran a calculator and found **the difference between working on and retiring outright was small**, so he decided to just retire. That's the method in action: **rather than switch to another job that's still selling your life for money, do the math first — often you can already stop.**[^1]

## Should I invest full-time?

Yes, but see it clearly for what it is. 00570's **Eason** went from a 9-to-5 worker to a "free man," making investing his whole vocation, finding it more interesting than his old job ("you can make money *and* make friends"). **Michael** could already retire but keeps working because it's interesting and helps people — just with less pressure and higher quality — **not for the money**.[^1]

The point isn't the title "living off investing," but this: investing itself **shouldn't become a new job to grind** (never sell, buy and sleep — it doesn't need you watching the screen daily). If full-time investing turns into another anxious rat race, it defeats the purpose.[^1]

## Toxic workplace? Leave

CLEC treats a toxic work environment as a relationship worth leaving. In 00570, SF looked back and saw how frightening his company's "traditional bad Taiwanese labor-relations culture, the boot-licking workplace culture" was. The principle matches [[別為錢賣命|don't sell your life for money]] and the "leave toxic relationships" thread — if the environment is toxic, get out fast. (This variant leans more toward the relationships side; see the toxic-relationships topic under Relationships & Family.)[^1]

## A common sticking point: you're already there, you just don't dare

For many people the barrier **isn't financial — it's psychological.** The whole 00546 session is titled "**You could have retired long ago; you just don't dare.**" James lowered the threshold from "50× annual expenses" to "**15× is enough to retire**" (10× in high-dividend paying ~12% for cash flow + 5× in QQQ / a 4-3-3 growth mix) and said flatly — **"If you want to retire, then retire; be a bit braver. You don't have to save up to 30× or 50×."** Once the numbers clear the threshold, all that's often left is the "I don't dare" hurdle — and that one is cleared by [[別為錢賣命|don't sell your life for money]] (ten more years alive beats ten more years working).[^3]

## Answers over time

- **2026-06-20 (00570)**: SF (42, technician) found switching jobs vs. retiring differed little → retired outright; Eason went full-time-investing as a "free man"; Michael kept working after financial freedom, but for interest and helping others, not money. James echoed 00568/00569's "past the threshold, stop selling your life for money."[^1]
- **2025-12-27 (00546)**: the very theme is "you could have retired long ago; you just don't dare" — lowered the threshold to 15× annual expenses (10× high-dividend + 5× growth), "if you want to retire, then retire, be braver." Points out the barrier is often psychological, not financial.[^3]

> ⚠️ "Can I quit" is a pure financial judgment that depends on your assets, cash flow, and local tax regime — this page is education only, not personalized career or retirement advice. Work out the numbers in [[退休需要幾倍年開銷|the retirement multiple]] and [[十五年現金流|15 years of cash flow]] first, then use [[別為錢賣命|don't sell your life for money]] to decide if it's worth it.
